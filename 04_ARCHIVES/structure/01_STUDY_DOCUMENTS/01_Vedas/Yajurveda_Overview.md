## Introduction

The Yajurveda, the "Veda of sacrificial formulas," is a compilation of prose mantras (*Yajus*) and verses recited by the *Adhvaryu* priest during the performance of *Yajnas* (sacrifices). It is the foundation of the *Karma Kanda* (section on ritual action) of the Vedic tradition and is unique for its division into two major recensions: the **Shukla Yajurveda (White Yajurveda)** and the **Krishna Yajurveda (Black Yajurveda)**.

## Core Philosophy

The core philosophy of the Yajurveda is centered on **Yajna** (sacrifice) as the mechanism for maintaining cosmic order (*Rta*) and achieving spiritual merit. The text marks a transition from the hymnal focus of the Rigveda to a more systematic and philosophical understanding of ritual. The later sections, particularly the **Isha Upanishad**, internalize the concept of sacrifice, shifting the focus from external rituals to inner, meditative practices.

## Structure

The Yajurveda is divided into two main branches:

| Feature | Shukla Yajurveda (White) | Krishna Yajurveda (Black) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Organization** | Clear separation of Mantras and Explanations. | Intermingling of Mantras and Explanations. |
| **Primary Samhita** | **Vajasaneyi Samhita** | **Taittiriya Samhita** |
| **Associated Upanishads** | **Isha Upanishad**, **Brihadaranyaka Upanishad** | **Taittiriya Upanishad**, **Katha Upanishad**, **Shvetashvatara Upanishad** |

## Key Verses

The Yajurveda contains some of the most profound verses in the Vedic tradition, particularly from the Isha Upanishad:
