## Introduction

In Chapter 14, Krishna explains the three modes (gunas) of material nature: sattva (goodness), rajas (passion), and tamas (ignorance). He describes how these modes influence the behavior and consciousness of the embodied soul, and how one can transcend them to attain liberation.

## Key Teachings

1.  **The Three Gunas**: Krishna explains that the entire material nature is composed of three modes: sattva, rajas, and tamas. These modes bind the eternal soul to the material body.
2.  **The Characteristics of the Gunas**:
    *   **Sattva (Goodness)**: The mode of goodness is illuminating and frees one from all sinful reactions. It binds the soul by a sense of happiness and knowledge.
    *   **Rajas (Passion)**: The mode of passion is born of unlimited desires and longings. It binds the soul by attachment to fruitive action.
    *   **Tamas (Ignorance)**: The mode of ignorance is the delusion of all embodied living entities. It binds the soul by madness, indolence, and sleep.
3.  **The Competition of the Gunas**: The three modes are in constant competition for supremacy. Sometimes the mode of goodness is prominent, sometimes passion, and sometimes ignorance.
4.  **Transcending the Gunas**: Krishna explains that one who is able to transcend the three modes is freed from birth, death, old age, and their distresses, and can enjoy nectar even in this life. Such a person is undisturbed by the reactions of the modes, is situated in the self, and sees a lump of earth, a stone, and a piece of gold with an equal eye.

## Key Themes

-   **Three Gunas (Modes)**: The fundamental forces of material nature that influence all aspects of life.
-   **Transcendence**: The process of rising above the influence of the three modes.
-   **Material Bondage**: The way in which the modes of nature bind the soul to the material world.
-   **Liberation**: The ultimate goal of transcending the modes and attaining the spiritual nature.

## Key Verse

> "When one is able to overcome the three modes of material nature, he can become free from birth, death, old age and their distresses and can enjoy nectar even in this life." (BG 14.20)

## Commentarial Perspectives

-   **Shankara (Advaita)**: Shankara interprets the three modes as the fundamental constituents of maya (illusion). He sees the chapter as a guide to understanding how the modes create the illusion of a separate self and how one can transcend them through knowledge.
-   **Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)**: Ramanuja sees the three modes as the qualities of prakriti (material nature), which is the body of God. He emphasizes that one can transcend the modes by taking refuge in God and engaging in devotional service.
-   **Madhva (Dvaita)**: Madhva interprets the three modes as the forces that bind the soul to the material world and obscure its true nature. He sees the chapter as a call to cultivate sattvic qualities and to seek the grace of God to overcome the influence of the modes.
