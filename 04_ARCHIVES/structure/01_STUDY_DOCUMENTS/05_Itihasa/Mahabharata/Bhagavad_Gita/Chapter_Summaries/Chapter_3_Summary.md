## Introduction

In Chapter 3, Arjuna expresses his confusion about Krishna's seemingly contradictory instructions to both renounce action and engage in action. Krishna resolves this by elaborating on the path of Karma Yoga, the yoga of selfless action, and explaining why it is a necessary and effective path to liberation for most people.

## Key Teachings

1.  **The Necessity of Action**: Krishna explains that no one can remain inactive even for a moment. Everyone is forced to act according to the qualities of their nature (the three gunas). Therefore, the path of renunciation of action is difficult and impractical for most.
2.  **Action without Attachment**: The key to Karma Yoga is to perform one's prescribed duties without attachment to the results. Such selfless action does not create karmic bondage and leads to purification of the mind.
3.  **Yajna (Sacrifice)**: Krishna introduces the concept of yajna (sacrifice) as a cosmic cycle. The gods are nourished by sacrifices, and in turn, they provide for the needs of humanity. Performing one's duties in a spirit of sacrifice is essential for maintaining cosmic order.
4.  **The Example of a Leader**: Krishna emphasizes that a wise person should act without attachment to set an example for others. The actions of a great person inspire the rest of society to follow the path of righteousness.
5.  **The Enemy of the Soul**: Krishna identifies lust (kama) and anger (krodha), born of the mode of passion (rajas), as the all-devouring enemies of the soul. He urges Arjuna to conquer this enemy through self-control and spiritual wisdom.

## Key Themes

-   **Selfless Action**: The core theme of the chapter is the performance of one's duty without attachment to the fruits of action.
-   **Sacrifice**: The concept of yajna as a means of maintaining cosmic order and purifying the mind.
-   **Cosmic Order**: The interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of fulfilling one's role in the cosmic cycle.
-   **Leadership**: The responsibility of a wise person to set an example for others.

## Key Verse

> "Whatever action a great person performs, common people follow in their footsteps. Whatever standards they set, the world pursues." (BG 3.21)

## Commentarial Perspectives

-   **Shankara (Advaita)**: Shankara interprets Karma Yoga as a preparatory path for the purification of the mind, which is essential for the attainment of knowledge (jnana). He maintains that liberation is ultimately achieved through knowledge alone.
-   **Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)**: Ramanuja sees Karma Yoga as an integral part of Bhakti Yoga. He argues that selfless action performed as an offering to God is a form of devotion that leads to God-realization.
-   **Madhva (Dvaita)**: Madhva emphasizes that all actions should be performed with the understanding that God is the ultimate doer and that the individual soul is merely an instrument. He sees Karma Yoga as a means of pleasing God and attaining His grace.
