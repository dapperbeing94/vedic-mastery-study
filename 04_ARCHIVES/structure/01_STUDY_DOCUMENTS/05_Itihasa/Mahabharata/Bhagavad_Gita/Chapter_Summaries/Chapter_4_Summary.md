## Introduction

In Chapter 4, Krishna reveals the ancient lineage of the yoga he is teaching and explains the nature of his divine incarnations. He elaborates on the relationship between action, inaction, and knowledge, showing how true wisdom transforms action into a spiritual practice.

## Key Teachings

1.  **The Lineage of Yoga**: Krishna reveals that he taught this imperishable science of yoga to the sun-god, Vivasvan, who in turn taught it to Manu, the father of mankind. This establishes the ancient and divine origin of the teachings.
2.  **Divine Incarnation (Avatara)**: Krishna explains that he incarnates himself in age after age whenever there is a decline of righteousness and a rise of unrighteousness, to protect the virtuous, annihilate the wicked, and reestablish dharma.
3.  **Action in Inaction, Inaction in Action**: A wise person sees inaction in action and action in inaction. This means understanding that the soul is not the doer, and that even in apparent inaction, the forces of nature are always at work.
4.  **The Fire of Knowledge**: Krishna uses the metaphor of a fire to explain the purifying power of knowledge. Just as a blazing fire turns firewood to ashes, so does the fire of knowledge burn to ashes all reactions to material activities.
5.  **The Importance of a Guru**: Krishna emphasizes the importance of approaching a self-realized spiritual master to receive transcendental knowledge. By rendering service and inquiring submissively, one can learn the truth from those who have seen it.

## Key Themes

-   **Divine Incarnation**: The concept of God descending into the material world to restore dharma.
-   **Sacred Knowledge**: The transformative power of spiritual knowledge to purify the mind and liberate the soul.
-   **Action in Knowledge**: The performance of action with the understanding that the soul is not the doer.
-   **Spiritual Teacher**: The necessity of a guru for attaining transcendental knowledge.

## Commentarial Perspectives

-   **Shankara (Advaita)**: Shankara emphasizes that true knowledge (jnana) is the direct means to liberation. He interprets the chapter as a guide to understanding the non-dual nature of reality, where all actions are ultimately seen as illusory from the standpoint of the Absolute.
-   **Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)**: Ramanuja highlights the importance of understanding Krishna's divine nature and his incarnations as a means of strengthening one's devotion. He sees the chapter as a call to perform all actions as a loving service to God.
-   **Madhva (Dvaita)**: Madhva focuses on the supreme and independent nature of God and the eternal dependence of the individual soul. He interprets the chapter as a revelation of God's divine power and grace, which are essential for liberation.
