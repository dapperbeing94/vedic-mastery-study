## 1. Introduction

**Adi Shankara** (c. 788-820 CE) was a seminal philosopher and theologian from India who consolidated the doctrine of **Advaita Vedanta**, a non-dualistic school of Hindu philosophy. He is revered as one of the most important thinkers in Indian history, and his works have had a profound and lasting impact on the intellectual and spiritual landscape of Hinduism.

## 2. Biographical Information

- **Birth**: Kalady, Kerala, India
- **Life**: Shankara is said to have been a child prodigy who mastered the Vedas and other scriptures at a very young age. He became a sannyasin (renunciate) and traveled extensively throughout India, engaging in debates with scholars of other philosophical schools and establishing monastic centers (mathas) to propagate his teachings.

## 3. Philosophical School: Advaita Vedanta

Advaita Vedanta is a non-dualistic school of Hindu philosophy that posits the ultimate reality (Brahman) as the only reality. The individual self (Atman) is identical with Brahman, and the perceived multiplicity of the world is an illusion (maya).

## 4. Major Works

Shankara is credited with writing commentaries (Bhashyas) on the ten principal Upanishads, the Brahma Sutras, and the Bhagavad Gita. He also wrote a number of independent philosophical works, including the **Vivekachudamani** and the **Upadesasahasri**.

## 5. Core Teachings

- **Brahman as the only reality**: The ultimate reality is Brahman, which is described as sat-chit-ananda (being-consciousness-bliss).
- **Atman is Brahman**: The individual self (Atman) is identical with Brahman.
- **Maya**: The perceived multiplicity of the world is an illusion (maya) that is superimposed on Brahman.
- **Moksha**: Liberation (moksha) is the realization of the identity of Atman and Brahman, which can be achieved through the path of knowledge (jnana yoga).

## 6. Key Contributions

- **Consolidation of Advaita Vedanta**: Shankara systematized and consolidated the teachings of Advaita Vedanta, making it one of the most influential schools of Hindu philosophy.
- **Establishment of monastic centers**: Shankara established four monastic centers (mathas) in the four corners of India, which have played a crucial role in the preservation and propagation of his teachings.

## 7. Significance

Adi Shankara is a towering figure in the history of Indian philosophy. His teachings have had a profound and lasting impact on the intellectual and spiritual landscape of Hinduism, and he is revered as a saint and a philosopher by millions of people around the world.

## 8. Relationship to Other Traditions

Shankara engaged in debates with scholars of other philosophical schools, including Buddhism and Mimamsa. He was a fierce critic of what he saw as the nihilism of Buddhism and the ritualism of Mimamsa.
