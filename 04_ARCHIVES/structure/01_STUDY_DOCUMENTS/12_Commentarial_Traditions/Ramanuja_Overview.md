## 1. Introduction

**Ramanuja** (c. 1017-1137 CE) was a prominent Hindu theologian, philosopher, and social reformer. He is the most important exponent of the **Vishishtadvaita** (qualified non-dualism) school of Vedanta, which holds that the ultimate reality (Brahman) is a personal God (Vishnu) who is both transcendent and immanent.

## 2. Biographical Information

- **Birth**: Sriperumbudur, Tamil Nadu, India
- **Life**: Ramanuja was a student of Yadava Prakasha, but he later broke with his teacher and developed his own philosophical system. He traveled extensively throughout India, engaging in debates with scholars of other philosophical schools and propagating his teachings.

## 3. Philosophical School: Vishishtadvaita

Vishishtadvaita is a qualified non-dualistic school of Vedanta that holds that the ultimate reality (Brahman) is a personal God (Vishnu) who is both transcendent and immanent. The individual self (Atman) is a part of Brahman, but it is not identical with Brahman.

## 4. Major Works

Ramanuja wrote commentaries on the Brahma Sutras (the **Sri Bhashya**), the Bhagavad Gita, and the Upanishads. He also wrote a number of independent philosophical works, including the **Vedarthasangraha**.

## 5. Core Teachings

- **Brahman as a personal God**: The ultimate reality is a personal God (Vishnu) who is the creator, preserver, and destroyer of the universe.
- **Atman as a part of Brahman**: The individual self (Atman) is a part of Brahman, but it is not identical with Brahman.
- **Bhakti**: Liberation (moksha) can be achieved through the path of devotion (bhakti) to Vishnu.

## 6. Key Contributions

- **Systematization of Vishishtadvaita**: Ramanuja systematized and consolidated the teachings of Vishishtadvaita, making it one of the most influential schools of Hindu philosophy.
- **Social reform**: Ramanuja was a social reformer who challenged the caste system and advocated for the inclusion of all people in the worship of Vishnu.

## 7. Significance

Ramanuja is a towering figure in the history of Hindu philosophy. His teachings have had a profound and lasting impact on the intellectual and spiritual landscape of Hinduism, and he is revered as a saint and a philosopher by millions of people around the world.

## 8. Relationship to Other Traditions

Ramanuja engaged in debates with scholars of other philosophical schools, including Advaita Vedanta and Jainism. He was a critic of the impersonalism of Advaita Vedanta and the atheism of Jainism.
