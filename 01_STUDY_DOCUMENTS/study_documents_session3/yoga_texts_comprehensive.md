# Yoga Texts - Comprehensive Study

## Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā (Light on Haṭha Yoga)

**Author/Tradition**: Yogi Svātmārāma (or Swatmarama), a disciple of Gorakshanath, belonging to the **Natha tradition** (or Nath Sampradaya). The text is dedicated to the guru Matsyendranath and Adinath (Shiva).

**Historical Period**: Composed in Sanskrit, the text is generally dated to the **15th century CE** (c. 1350–1500 CE). This places it in the medieval period of Indian history, a time when the practice of Hatha Yoga was being systematically codified.

### Core Teachings
The Hatha Yoga Pradipika's central teaching is that **Haṭha Yoga is a preparatory step for Rājayoga** (the yoga of mental control and absorption). The text's primary goal is to achieve **Samādhi** (a state of meditative absorption or spiritual liberation) by awakening the **Kuṇḍalinī** energy. This is done through the purification of the body and the control of *prāṇa* (life force). The term *Haṭha* itself is interpreted as the union of the two opposing energies: *Ha* (sun, representing the vital force, *prāṇa*) and *Ṭha* (moon, representing the eliminative force, *apāna*), which are balanced to allow the *prāṇa* to flow through the central psychic channel, the **Suṣumnā Nādī**. The text emphasizes the importance of a proper environment, diet, and the guidance of a guru.

### Key Concepts
**Haṭha** (हठ): The forceful or determined effort; also the union of *Ha* (sun/prāṇa) and *Ṭha* (moon/apāna).
**Kuṇḍalinī** (कुण्डलिनी): The coiled, dormant spiritual energy at the base of the spine, which the practices aim to awaken.
**Suṣumnā Nādī** (सुषुम्ना नाडी): The central psychic channel running through the spine, through which the awakened *Kuṇḍalinī* ascends.
**Prāṇāyāma** (प्राणायाम): Breath control techniques for regulating *prāṇa* (life force).
**Mudrā** (मुद्रा) and **Bandha** (बन्ध): Psychic gestures (seals) and energy locks used to contain and direct *prāṇa*.
**Ṣaṭkarma** (षट्कर्म): The six purification techniques (e.g., *Neti*, *Dhauti*) to cleanse the body before *Prāṇāyāma*.
**Samādhi** (समाधि): The state of meditative absorption, the ultimate goal of the practice.
**Laya Yoga** (लय योग): The yoga of dissolution or absorption, which is achieved through the practices.

### Structure
The text is organized into **four chapters**, known as *Upadeśas* (instructions or discourses), containing approximately 389 *ślokas* (verses):
1.  **Prathama Upadeśa (First Chapter - Āsana):** Focuses on the proper environment for a yogi, ethical observances, and the practice of *Āsana* (postures), detailing 15 specific postures.
2.  **Dvitīya Upadeśa (Second Chapter - Ṣaṭkarma and Prāṇāyāma):** Describes the six purification techniques (*Ṣaṭkarma*) and various *Prāṇāyāma* (breath control) methods, emphasizing the cleansing of the *nāḍīs* (energy channels).
3.  **Tṛtīya Upadeśa (Third Chapter - Mudrā and Bandha):** Details the ten *Mudrās* (seals) and *Bandhas* (locks) which are essential for awakening the *Kuṇḍalinī* and achieving *Samādhi*.
4.  **Caturtha Upadeśa (Fourth Chapter - Samādhi):** Describes the process of *Samādhi* (meditative absorption), the goal of Hatha Yoga, and the concept of *Nādānusandhāna* (listening to the inner sound) as a means to achieve it.

### Practical Applications
The text provides a systematic, step-by-step methodology for spiritual practice, focusing on physical mastery as a prerequisite for mental mastery.
1.  **Physical Purification:** Practices like *Ṣaṭkarma* are used to cleanse the internal organs and nervous system, making the body fit for higher practices.
2.  **Postural Stability (*Āsana*):** The postures are taught to bring steadiness, health, and lightness to the body, enabling the practitioner to sit for long periods in meditation.
3.  **Energy Control (*Prāṇāyāma*):** Breath control is used to stabilize the mind, as the text states, "When the breath moves, the mind moves; when the breath is still, the mind is still."
4.  **Subtle Energy Management (*Mudrā* and *Bandha*):** These practices are applied to awaken the *Kuṇḍalinī* and force the *prāṇa* into the *Suṣumnā Nādī*, leading directly to the state of *Samādhi*.
5.  **Dietary and Environmental Guidance:** The text includes specific instructions on *Mita-āhāra* (moderate, wholesome diet) and the ideal environment for a yogi, ensuring a conducive lifestyle for spiritual growth.

### Key Verses/Passages
**Verse 1.1:**
*Sanskrit:* श्री आदिनाथाय नमोऽस्तु तस्मै येनोपदिष्टा हठयोगविद्या।
विभ्राजते प्रोन्नतराजयोगमारोढुमिच्छोरधिरोहिणीव॥
*Translation:* "Salutations to Ādinātha (Śiva), who first taught the science of Haṭhayoga. It shines forth as a staircase for those who wish to ascend to the heights of Rājayoga." (This verse establishes the text's purpose: Hatha Yoga is a means to Raja Yoga.)

**Verse 2.66:**
*Sanskrit:* यावद्वायुः स्थितो देहे तावज्जीवनमुच्यते।
मरणं तस्य निष्क्रान्तिस्ततो वायुं निरुन्धयेत्॥
*Translation:* "As long as the breath remains in the body, so long is there life. The departure of the breath is death. Therefore, one should restrain the breath." (This highlights the importance of *Prāṇāyāma* for longevity and spiritual progress.)

### Cross-References
The Hatha Yoga Pradipika is a pivotal text that bridges the gap between the earlier classical Yoga of Patañjali's **Yoga Sūtras** and the later, more physically-oriented Hatha Yoga traditions. Svātmārāma explicitly states that the purpose of Hatha Yoga is to attain Rājayoga, thus positioning his work as a practical method to achieve the goal outlined in the Yoga Sūtras. It also draws heavily from the teachings of the **Natha Sampradaya** and is often grouped with other classical Hatha Yoga texts like the **Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā** and the **Śiva Saṁhitā**. It references the concept of *samādhi* found in the **Upaniṣads** and the **Bhagavad Gītā**, but provides a distinct, systematic methodology involving physical practices to achieve it.

### Authoritative Sources
**Primary Text:** *Haṭhayogapradīpikā* by Svātmārāma.
**Key Commentaries/Translations:**
*   *Jyotsnā* commentary by Brahmānanda.
*   Translation and commentary by Swami Muktibodhananda (Bihar School of Yoga).
*   Translation and commentary by Brian Dana Akers.
*   Scholarly works by James Mallinson and Mark Singleton, who have extensively researched the history and practice of Hatha Yoga.

## Yoga Vasistha (योगवासिष्ठम्)

**Author/Tradition**: Traditionally attributed to Maharishi Valmiki, but the actual author is unknown. It is a key text in the Advaita Vedanta tradition, also incorporating influences from Kashmiri Shaivism.

**Historical Period**: The text evolved over several centuries, with the earliest version (Mokṣopāya) dated to the 10th century CE in Kashmir, and the final, complete text reaching its current form between the 10th and 14th centuries CE.

### Core Teachings
The core teaching is **Advaita Vedanta** (non-dualism), emphasizing that the world is a mere appearance or **Maya** (illusion) within the ultimate reality, **Brahman**. It teaches that liberation (**Moksha**) is achieved through self-effort (**Puruṣārtha**) and the realization of the non-dual nature of the Self (**Atman**) and Brahman. It is a philosophical dialogue that systematically presents the path to self-realization.

### Key Concepts
Key concepts include: **Moksha** (liberation); **Maya** (illusion, the apparent reality of the world); **Brahman** (the ultimate, non-dual reality); **Jivanmukta** (liberated while living); **Puruṣārtha** (self-effort, the power of human will over destiny); **Samsara** (the cycle of birth and death); **Manas** (mind, which is the source of all suffering and creation); **Sankalpa** (thought or intention, the root of the mind's activity).

### Structure
The complete text, also known as **Brihat Yoga Vasistha**, contains over 29,000 verses and is structured as a discourse between the sage **Vasistha** and Prince **Rama**. It is divided into six books (**Prakaraṇams**): 1. **Vairagya** (Dispassion), 2. **Mumukṣu Vyavahāra** (Behavior of the Seeker), 3. **Utpatti** (Origin/Creation), 4. **Sthiti** (Existence/Preservation), 5. **Upashama** (Dissolution/Quiescence), and 6. **Nirvana** (Liberation).

### Practical Applications
The text is highly practical, focusing on the power of **Puruṣārtha** (self-effort) and the cultivation of a tranquil mind. It guides the seeker toward the state of **Jivanmukta** (liberated while living) by teaching them to view the world as a dream-like appearance, thereby dissolving mental suffering and attachment. It encourages constant self-inquiry and meditation on the non-dual Self.

### Key Verses/Passages
A key verse illustrating the text's philosophy of creation and mind is: **'Manah eva jagat sarvam'** (The mind alone is the entire world). Another central teaching is: **'Yatha-icchasi tatha kuru'** (As you desire, so you do), which emphasizes the supremacy of self-effort (Puruṣārtha) over fate (Daiva). The text repeatedly stresses that the world is a mere projection of the mind, and liberation is the cessation of mental activity (Sankalpa).

### Cross-References
It is closely aligned with **Advaita Vedanta** philosophy. It is presented as a dialogue within the framework of the **Ramayana**, with Sage Vasistha instructing Prince Rama. It is also known by other names such as **Maha-Ramayana** and **Vasiṣṭha Ramayana**. Scholars note that it incorporates and synthesizes ideas from various Indian philosophical traditions, including early **Upanishads**, **Buddhism** (Vijnanavada and Madhyamaka schools), and **Kashmiri Shaivism** (Trika school).

### Authoritative Sources
Key sources and scholarly interpretations include: **Vasistha's Yoga** (translation by Swami Venkatesananda), **The Yoga-Vasistha-Maharamayana** (translation by Vihari Lal Mitra), and scholarly works by **Christopher Chapple**, **B.L. Atreya** (The Philosophy of the Yogavasistha), and **Walter Slaje** (on the dating and development of the Mokṣopāya). The text itself is often referred to as an 'encyclopedia of Advaita Vedanta'.

## Gheranda Samhita (Gheraṇḍa-saṃhitā)

**Author/Tradition**: Sage Gheranda (preceptor) and Chanda Kapali (pupil). The text belongs to the tradition of Hatha Yoga, specifically *Ghaṭastha Yoga* (vessel yoga), which focuses on purifying and strengthening the physical body as a vessel for the soul.

**Historical Period**: Late **17th century CE** (circa 1700 CE). It is a relatively late text in the Hatha Yoga tradition, likely composed in the Bengal region of India.

### Core Teachings
The core teaching is the sevenfold path of *Ghaṭastha Yoga* (vessel yoga), which aims at the purification and strengthening of the body and mind to achieve the ultimate goal of *Samādhi* (ultimate absorption). Unlike the eight limbs of Patañjali's Yoga Sutras, the *Gheranda Samhita* focuses heavily on physical and physiological practices. The text emphasizes that the body (*ghaṭa*) must be purified and made firm before the higher stages of yoga can be attained. The philosophical underpinning is rooted in the Advaita Vedanta and Tantra traditions, underscoring the unity of the individual soul (*Ātman*) with the Universal Consciousness (*Brahman*).

### Key Concepts
*   **Saptānga Yoga** (Seven-limbed Yoga): The unique sevenfold path of the text.
*   **Ghaṭastha Yoga** (Vessel Yoga): The yoga of the body as a vessel, emphasizing physical purification.
*   **Shodhana** (Purification): The first limb, comprising 6 cleansing acts (*Shatkarmas*).
*   **Āsana** (Posture): The second limb, detailing 32 specific postures.
*   **Mudra** (Seal/Energy Control): The third limb, describing 25 techniques for directing vital energy.
*   **Pratyāhāra** (Sense Withdrawal): The fourth limb, focusing on controlling the mind by withdrawing the senses.
*   **Prāṇāyāma** (Breath Control): The fifth limb, detailing 10 types of breath control.
*   **Dhyāna** (Meditation): The sixth limb, describing three types of meditation.
*   **Samādhi** (Ultimate Absorption): The seventh limb, describing six ways to attain the state of ultimate liberation.

### Structure
The text is structured as a dialogue between the sage Gheranda and his disciple Chanda Kapali, and is divided into **seven chapters** (*Upadeśas*), outlining the **Saptānga Yoga** (Seven-limbed Yoga) path:
1.  **Shodhana** (Purification): Describes 6 cleansing acts (*Shatkarmas*).
2.  **Āsana** (Posture): Describes 32 postures.
3.  **Mudra** (Seal/Energy Control): Describes 25 *mudrās*.
4.  **Pratyāhāra** (Sense Withdrawal): Describes the technique for sense withdrawal.
5.  **Prāṇāyāma** (Breath Control): Describes 10 types of breath control.
6.  **Dhyāna** (Meditation): Describes 3 types of meditation.
7.  **Samādhi** (Ultimate Absorption): Describes 6 ways to attain *Samādhi*.
The text contains a total of **351 *shlokas*** (verses).

### Practical Applications
The text is a practical manual for Hatha Yoga practitioners, providing detailed instructions for physical and energetic mastery.
*   **Physical Health and Purification**: The extensive section on *Shodhana* (cleansing) and *Āsana* (postures) provides a systematic approach to detoxifying and strengthening the physical body, which is seen as the foundation for spiritual practice.
*   **Energy and Mind Control**: The practices of *Mudra* and *Prāṇāyāma* are applied to awaken and control the vital life force (*prāṇa*), leading to mental stability and clarity.
*   **Spiritual Liberation**: The final limbs, *Dhyāna* and *Samādhi*, offer various meditative techniques, including *Bhakti-yoga-samādhi* (devotional absorption) and *Rāja-yoga-samādhi* (royal absorption), providing multiple paths for the spiritual seeker to achieve union with the Divine.

### Key Verses/Passages
**Verse 1.4 (on the nature of liberation):**
> *Nāsti māyā-samaḥ pāśo, na ca yogāt paraṁ balam.*
> *Na hi jñānāt paro bandhur, nāhaṅkārāt paro ripuḥ.*
>
> **Translation:**
> There are no fetters like those of illusion (*māyā*),
> no strength like that which comes from discipline (*yoga*),
> there is no friend higher than knowledge (*jñāna*),
> and no greater enemy than egoism (*ahaṅkāra*).
>
> **Verse 1.10 (on the purpose of Ghaṭastha Yoga):**
> *Ghaṭasya rañjanaṁ caiva, ghaṭasya sthāpanaṁ tathā.*
> *Ghaṭasya śodhanaṁ caiva, ghaṭasya dṛḍhatā tathā.*
>
> **Translation:**
> The body is to be nourished, the body is to be made firm,
> the body is to be purified, and the body is to be strengthened.

### Cross-References
The *Gheranda Samhita* is one of the three classic texts of Hatha Yoga, alongside the **Hatha Yoga Pradipika** (15th century CE) and the **Shiva Samhita** (15th-17th century CE). It is distinguished by its unique **sevenfold path** (*Saptānga Yoga*), in contrast to the four-limbed approach of the *Hatha Yoga Pradipika* and the six-limbed approach of the *Shiva Samhita*. It is considered the most encyclopedic of the Hatha Yoga texts due to its detailed enumeration of practices, such as 32 *āsanas* and 25 *mudrās*. The text also draws on earlier traditions, with some verses being directly borrowed or adapted from texts like the *Goraksha Samhita*.

## Shiva Samhita

**Author/Tradition**: Tantric Hatha Yoga tradition. The text is presented as a dialogue between Lord Shiva and his consort Parvati. The author is unknown, though some traditions attribute it to Matsyendra.

**Historical Period**: The text's composition period is debated, with some scholars suggesting a date as early as 1500 BC. However, the consensus among modern scholars places its composition much later, most likely in the **17th or 18th century AD**.

### Core Teachings
The Shiva Samhita is a comprehensive treatise on Hatha Yoga and Tantra, focusing on the ultimate goal of **Jñāna** (eternal true knowledge) and liberation from the cycle of birth and death. It asserts a non-dualistic (**Advaita**) philosophical view, where **Caitanya Ātmā** (divine consciousness) is the sole reality, and the individual soul (**Jīva**) is trapped in the delusion (**Māyā**) of the world. The text systematically outlines four paths of Yoga—Mantra, Hatha, Laya, and Raja—and provides detailed instructions for practical techniques, emphasizing the importance of a proper **Guru** and overcoming worldly attachments (**bhoga**) and excessive ritualism (**dharma**).

### Key Concepts
**Jñāna** (Eternal true knowledge), **Jīva/Jīvātmā** (Individual soul/vital principle), **Māyā** (Delusion/illusion), **Caitanya Ātmā** (Divine consciousness), **Nāḍī** (Subtle energy channels), **Kundalinī** (Coiled energy), **Vāyu/Prāṇa** (Vital airs/life force), **Prāṇāyāma** (Breath control), **Nāḍīśodhana** (Nerve purification), **Siddhi** (Supernatural powers), **Mudrā** (Seals/gestures), **Āsana** (Postures), **Cakra** (Energy centers), **Pratyāhāra** (Sense withdrawal), **Dhāraṇā** (Concentration), **Kriyāyoga** (A mysterious technique), **Japa** (Mantra repetition).

### Structure
The Shiva Samhita is a text of 642 verses divided into five chapters (**Pañcapaṭala**):
1.  **Prathamapaṭala:** Focuses on **Jñāna** (knowledge) and philosophical perspectives, including the nature of the **Jīva**.
2.  **Dvitīyapaṭala:** Describes the subtle body, including **Nāḍī**, **Kundalinī**, and the workings of the **Jīvātmā**.
3.  **Tṛtīyapaṭala:** Details the ten **Vāyu** (Prāṇa), the role of the **Guru**, **Prāṇāyāma**, **Nāḍīśodhana**, four stages of yoga, eight **Siddhi**, and four **Āsanas**.
4.  **Caturthapaṭala:** Explains the powers of ten **Mudrā** (seals/gestures) and the resulting yogic attainments.
5.  **Pañcamapaṭala:** The longest chapter, which discusses obstacles to liberation, the four types of aspirants, and advanced practices like **Pratyāhāra**, **Dhāraṇā**, **Cakra** meditation, and **Pratikopasana** (shadow gazing).

### Practical Applications
The text provides a practical framework for spiritual development by categorizing aspirants into four types (mild, moderate, ardent, most ardent) and assigning them to the four paths of yoga (Mantra, Laya, Hatha, Raja). It details specific techniques such as **Nāḍīśodhana** (nerve purification), **Prāṇāyāma** (breath control), **Mudrā** (seals), **Āsana** (postures), and the esoteric practice of **Pratikopasana** (shadow gazing) for achieving spiritual goals. Crucially, it emphasizes the necessity of cultivating ethical qualities (as described in the profile of the **Adhimatra** aspirant) and warns against the distractions of worldly enjoyments (**bhoga**) and ritualistic practices (**dharma**).

### Key Verses/Passages
**Verse 5.9 (Four Kinds of Yoga):**
*   **Sanskrit (implied):** *Mantra-yoga, haṭha-yoga, laya-yoga, rāja-yoga*
*   **Translation:** "The Yoga is of four kinds: First **mantrayoga**, second **hathayoga**, third **layayoga**, fourth **rajayoga**, which discards duality."

**Verse 5.16 (Invocation of the Shadow - Pratikopasana):**
*   **Translation:** "In a clear sun-lit sky, behold with a steady gaze your own divine reflection; whenever this is seen even for a single second in the sky, you behold God at once in the sky."

### Cross-References
The Shiva Samhita is one of the three major classical texts on Hatha Yoga, alongside the **Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā** and the **Haṭhayoga Pradīpikā**. It is known to borrow certain features and even verses verbatim from the **Haṭhayoga Pradīpikā**. It is also grouped with the **Gorakṣa Śataka** and **Siddha Siddhānta Padhdati** as one of the five basic Hatha Yoga texts.

### Authoritative Sources

*   *Siva Samhita Sanskrit text with English translation* by Srisa Chandra Vasu (an older, influential translation).
*   Scholarly articles from journals like *Ancient Science of Life*.

## Goraksha Shataka (Gorakṣaśataka)

**Author/Tradition**: Attributed to the sage Gorakshanath (Gorakṣa), a foundational figure of the Nath tradition (Nath Sampradaya).

**Historical Period**: Generally dated to the 11th-12th century CE, making it one of the earliest known texts on Hatha Yoga.

### Core Teachings
The text is a foundational treatise on Hatha Yoga, focusing on the six-limbed (Shadanga) system of practice. It details techniques for physical and energetic control to achieve spiritual liberation. Key elements include the awakening of Kundalini Shakti, the control of Prana, and the union of Shiva and Shakti within the body. It emphasizes the importance of the Guru (Matsyendranath is invoked) and the attainment of the highest state (paramampadam) through yogic discipline.

### Key Concepts
Shadanga Yoga (six limbs of yoga: asana, pranayama, pratyahara, dharana, dhyana, samadhi); Bandhas (Mula Bandha, Uddiyana Bandha, Jalandhara Bandha); Mudras (Khechari Mudra, Mahamudra); Prana (life force); Sushumna Nadi (central energy channel); Kundalini Shakti (serpent power); Paramampadam (the highest state/liberation).

### Structure
The title means 'One Hundred Verses of Goraksha' (Shataka = 100). It is a short, concise text composed of approximately 100-101 Sanskrit verses (shlokas). The text is organized around the description of the six limbs of yoga (Shadanga Yoga) and the specific practices associated with them, such as asanas, bandhas, and mudras.

### Practical Applications
The text provides practical instructions for physical and mental purification and control. It details specific techniques like the three primary Bandhas and Mudras, which are essential for controlling the flow of Prana and awakening the Kundalini. The practices are aimed at preparing the body and mind for the higher stages of meditation (dhyana) and final absorption (samadhi), leading to liberation (moksha).

### Key Verses/Passages
Verse 1 (Invocation): 'sriguram paramdnandam vande svanandavigraham, Yasya sdmnidhyamdtrena cidanandayate tanuh.' (I bow down to the venerable guru Matsyendranath (who is) supreme bliss, embodiment of his own bliss; simply by means of proximity to whom the body becomes nothing but knowledge and bliss.)\n\nVerse 2 (Purpose): 'Goraksah satakam vakti yoginam hitakamyaya, Dhruvam yasyavabodhena jdyate paramampadam.' (With desire for the benefit of Yogis, Goraksa proclaims (the) One Hundred (verses) by the knowledge of which is surely brought about the highest state.)\n\nVerse 10 (Siddhasana): 'Yoni-sthanam-anghri-mulaka-ghatitam, me'dhra-mule nyaset, me'dhre me'dhra-mule nyaset, siddhasanam-idam prachakshate.' (Placing the heel of the foot at the perineum, and the other heel above the lingam, this is called Siddhasana.)

### Cross-References
The Goraksha Shataka is considered a precursor to the later, more famous Hatha Yoga texts. It is frequently referenced alongside the *Hatha Yoga Pradipika* (15th century) and the *Gheranda Samhita* (17th century), which elaborate on the practices it introduces. Its six-limbed system (*Shadanga Yoga*) contrasts with the eight-limbed system (*Ashtanga Yoga*) of Patanjali's *Yoga Sutras*, though both share the ultimate goal of *samadhi*. It is a key text for understanding the development of the Nath Sampradaya's unique yogic tradition.

### Authoritative Sources
Scholarly works and critical editions, including those by Swami Kuvalayananda and S. A. Shukla, are authoritative. Other key sources include academic studies on the Nath tradition and the history of Hatha Yoga, such as those found in the *Journal of Yoga and Meditation* and various university publications on Indian philosophy and religion.



## Shiva Samhita (Śiva-saṃhitā)

**Author/Tradition**: The author is unknown, but the text is presented as a discourse from Lord Shiva to his consort Parvati. It is a key text in the Hatha Yoga and Tantra traditions, with a strong emphasis on non-dual (Advaita) philosophy.

**Historical Period**: The text is estimated to have been composed between the **15th and 17th centuries CE**, likely in the 17th century. It is considered one of the three classic texts of Hatha Yoga, along with the *Hatha Yoga Pradipika* and the *Gheranda Samhita*.

### Core Teachings
The *Shiva Samhita* presents a comprehensive and deeply philosophical approach to Hatha Yoga. Its core teachings include:
*   **Advaita Vedanta Philosophy:** The text strongly emphasizes the non-dual nature of reality, stating that the individual soul (*jiva*) is identical to the Supreme Self (*Brahman*). Liberation (*moksha*) is the realization of this unity.
*   **The Subtle Body:** It provides a detailed description of the subtle body, including the *nadis* (energy channels), *chakras* (energy centers), and the awakening of *Kundalini*.
*   **The Importance of the Guru:** The text stresses the necessity of a qualified guru for success in yoga.
*   **Yoga for Householders:** Unlike many other yoga texts, the *Shiva Samhita* explicitly states that its teachings are for all sincere seekers, including householders, and not just for ascetics.
*   **Integration of Jnana, Bhakti, and Karma Yoga:** The text integrates the paths of knowledge (*jnana*), devotion (*bhakti*), and action (*karma*) within the framework of Hatha Yoga.

### Key Concepts
*   **Jiva and Brahman:** The individual soul and the Supreme Self, which are ultimately one.
*   **Kundalini:** The dormant spiritual energy at the base of the spine, which is to be awakened and guided upward through the *sushumna nadi*.
*   **Nadis:** The subtle energy channels in the body, of which the three most important are *Ida*, *Pingala*, and *Sushumna*.
*   **Chakras:** The subtle energy centers along the spine, which are pierced by the awakened Kundalini.
*   **Prana:** The vital life force, which is controlled through *pranayama*.
*   **Moksha:** Liberation from the cycle of birth and death, which is the ultimate goal of yoga.
*   **Vajroli Mudra:** A controversial and advanced practice for the sublimation of sexual energy.

### Structure
The *Shiva Samhita* is divided into five chapters (*patalas*):
1.  **Chapter 1: The Nature of Reality:** This chapter lays the philosophical foundation of the text, discussing the nature of the Self, liberation, and the importance of yoga.
2.  **Chapter 2: The Subtle Body:** This chapter provides a detailed description of the *nadis*, *chakras*, and the *jiva*.
3.  **Chapter 3: Prana and Pranayama:** This chapter discusses the vital airs (*pranas*) and the practice of *pranayama* (breath control).
4.  **Chapter 4: Mudras:** This chapter describes various *mudras* (seals) for awakening Kundalini and controlling vital energy.
5.  **Chapter 5: The Path to Liberation:** This chapter discusses the different types of yoga, the obstacles to yoga, and the signs of success on the path.

### Practical Applications
The *Shiva Samhita* offers a variety of practical techniques for spiritual aspirants, including:
*   **Meditation:** The text emphasizes meditation on the Self as the primary means of liberation.
*   **Pranayama:** Various breath control techniques are described for purifying the *nadis* and controlling *prana*.
*   **Mudras:** A number of *mudras* are detailed for awakening Kundalini and directing energy.
*   **Visualization:** The text encourages visualization of the subtle body and the chakras.
*   **Ethical Conduct:** The text emphasizes the importance of ethical conduct and a righteous lifestyle for success in yoga.

### Key Verses/Passages
**Verse 1.1:**
*Sanskrit:* एकदा तु महादेवो विजने पर्वतात्मजाम्। पप्रच्छ पार्वती देवी लोकानां हितकाम्यया॥
*Translation:* "Once, in a secluded place, for the welfare of the worlds, the goddess Parvati asked Mahadeva (Shiva)."

**Verse 2.1:**
*Sanskrit:* अस्मिन्देहे स्थिते देवि सर्वं विश्वं प्रतिष्ठितम्। तस्मिन्स्थिते स्थिते देवि सर्वं विश्वं प्रतिष्ठितम्॥
*Translation:* "O Goddess, in this body, the entire universe is established. In it being established, O Goddess, the entire universe is established."

### Cross-References
The *Shiva Samhita* is one of the three classic texts of Hatha Yoga, along with the **Hatha Yoga Pradipika** and the **Gheranda Samhita**. It is considered the most philosophical and comprehensive of the three. It shares many practices with the other two texts, but it places a greater emphasis on the philosophical underpinnings of yoga, particularly the non-dual philosophy of **Advaita Vedanta**. The text also shows strong influences from the **Tantra** tradition, particularly in its emphasis on the subtle body and the awakening of Kundalini.

## Goraksha Shataka (Gorakṣaśataka)

**Author/Tradition**: Attributed to Gorakshanath (also known as Gorakhnath), the revered founder of the Nath tradition of Hatha Yoga.

**Historical Period**: The text is believed to have been composed between the **10th and 13th centuries CE**, making it one of the earliest and most influential texts on Hatha Yoga.

### Core Teachings
The *Goraksha Shataka* is a concise and authoritative text that lays out the fundamental principles and practices of Hatha Yoga. Its core teachings include:
*   **The Six Limbs of Yoga:** The text presents a six-limbed system of yoga: *asana* (posture), *pranayama* (breath control), *pratyahara* (sense withdrawal), *dharana* (concentration), *dhyana* (meditation), and *samadhi* (ultimate absorption).
*   **The Awakening of Kundalini:** The primary goal of the practices is to awaken the dormant spiritual energy of Kundalini and guide it upward through the central channel (*sushumna nadi*) to unite with Shiva in the crown chakra (*sahasrara*).
*   **The Importance of the Guru:** The text emphasizes the necessity of a qualified guru for success in yoga.
*   **The Purification of the Nadis:** The text stresses the importance of purifying the energy channels (*nadis*) through *pranayama* before attempting to awaken Kundalini.

### Key Concepts
*   **Asana:** The text describes two primary postures: *siddhasana* (adept's pose) and *padmasana* (lotus pose).
*   **Pranayama:** The text details various breath control techniques, including *nadi shodhana* (alternate nostril breathing) and the three *bandhas* (*mula*, *uddiyana*, and *jalandhara*).
*   **Kundalini:** The dormant spiritual energy at the base of the spine.
*   **Sushumna Nadi:** The central energy channel through which Kundalini ascends.
*   **Chakras:** The text describes the six main chakras along the spine.
*   **Samadhi:** The state of ultimate absorption, in which the individual self merges with the Supreme Self.

### Structure
The *Goraksha Shataka* is a short text consisting of approximately 100 verses (*shlokas*). It is organized into a single chapter that systematically presents the six limbs of yoga.

### Practical Applications
The *Goraksha Shataka* provides a clear and concise guide to the practice of Hatha Yoga. Its practical applications include:
*   **Asana:** The practice of *siddhasana* and *padmasana* to create a stable foundation for meditation.
*   **Pranayama:** The use of breath control techniques to purify the *nadis* and control *prana*.
*   **Bandhas:** The application of the three *bandhas* to lock and direct energy.
*   **Meditation:** The practice of meditation to quiet the mind and realize the Self.

### Key Verses/Passages
**Verse 4:**
*Sanskrit:* आसनं प्राणसंरोधः प्रत्याहारश्च धारणा। ध्यानं समाधिरेतानि योगाङ्गानि भवन्ति षट्॥
*Translation:* "Asana, pranayama, pratyahara, dharana, dhyana, and samadhi are the six limbs of yoga."

**Verse 100:**
*Sanskrit:* यत्र यत्र मनो याति तत्र तत्र परं पदम्। तत्र तत्र परं ब्रह्म सर्वत्र समवस्थितम्॥
*Translation:* "Wherever the mind goes, there is the supreme state. There is the supreme Brahman, established equally everywhere."

### Cross-References
The *Goraksha Shataka* is a foundational text of the **Nath tradition** and has had a profound influence on later Hatha Yoga texts, including the **Hatha Yoga Pradipika**. It is one of the earliest texts to systematically present the practices of Hatha Yoga. The text's six-limbed system of yoga is a variation of the eight-limbed system of **Patanjali's Yoga Sutras**, with a greater emphasis on the physical practices of *asana* and *pranayama*.
