## Introduction

The Bhagavad Gita, or "Song of God," is a 700-verse Hindu scripture that is part of the ancient Indian epic, the Mahabharata. It is a dialogue between the prince Arjuna and his charioteer, the god Krishna, on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. The Gita presents a synthesis of Hindu ideas about dharma, theistic bhakti, and the yogic paths to moksha (liberation).

## Core Philosophy

The central philosophy of the Bhagavad Gita is the reconciliation of the paths of **Karma Yoga** (the yoga of selfless action), **Jnana Yoga** (the yoga of knowledge), and **Bhakti Yoga** (the yoga of devotion). Krishna teaches Arjuna that one can achieve liberation by performing one's duties without attachment to the fruits of action, with a mind fixed on the divine.

## Structure

The Bhagavad Gita is composed of 18 chapters, which are traditionally divided into three sections of six chapters each:

| Section | Chapters | Yoga | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **I** | 1-6 | Karma Yoga | The Path of Action |
| **II** | 7-12 | Bhakti Yoga | The Path of Devotion |
| **III** | 13-18 | Jnana Yoga | The Path of Knowledge |

## Chapter Summaries

The following table provides a brief summary of each of the 18 chapters of the Bhagavad Gita:

| Chapter | Title | Summary |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Arjuna's Dilemma | Arjuna's moral crisis on the battlefield. |
| 2 | Transcendental Knowledge | Krishna's initial teachings on the nature of the soul and the body. |
| 3 | Path of Action | The importance of selfless action (Karma Yoga). |
| 4 | Divine Knowledge | The nature of divine incarnation and the importance of knowledge. |
| 5 | Renunciation of Action | The relationship between action and renunciation. |
| 6 | Meditation | Instructions on the practice of meditation (Dhyana Yoga). |
| 7 | Knowledge of the Absolute | Krishna's divine nature and energies. |
| 8 | The Imperishable Brahman | The nature of Brahman and the soul's journey after death. |
| 9 | Royal Knowledge | The most confidential knowledge of devotional service. |
| 10 | Divine Glories | Krishna's manifestations and glories. |
| 11 | Universal Form | Krishna's cosmic vision revealed to Arjuna. |
| 12 | Path of Devotion | The superiority of devotional service (Bhakti Yoga). |
| 13 | Nature and the Enjoyer | The difference between the body (the field) and the soul (the knower of the field). |
| 14 | Three Modes of Material Nature | The three gunas (sattva, rajas, tamas) and their influence. |
| 15 | The Supreme Person | Krishna as the Supreme Person (Purushottama). |
| 16 | Divine and Demonic Natures | The qualities of the divine and demonic natures. |
| 17 | Three Types of Faith | The influence of the three gunas on faith, worship, and austerity. |
| 18 | Liberation by Renunciation | A summary of all teachings and the ultimate instruction to surrender to God. |

## Commentarial Traditions

The Bhagavad Gita has been interpreted by the three major schools of Vedanta philosophy:

-   **Adi Shankaracharya (Advaita Vedanta)**: Emphasizes Jnana Yoga and the non-dual identity of the soul and Brahman.
-   **Ramanujacharya (Vishishtadvaita Vedanta)**: Emphasizes Bhakti Yoga and the qualified non-dualism of the soul and a personal God.
-   **Madhvacharya (Dvaita Vedanta)**: Emphasizes Bhakti Yoga and the eternal dualism of the soul and a personal God.
