## Introduction

In Chapter 16, Krishna describes the divine (daivi) and demonic (asuri) natures, outlining the qualities and characteristics of each. This chapter provides a clear moral framework for spiritual life, explaining the consequences of living a life of righteousness versus a life of ungodliness.

## Key Teachings

1.  **The Divine Qualities (Daivi Sampat)**: Krishna lists twenty-six divine qualities, including fearlessness, purification of one's existence, cultivation of spiritual knowledge, charity, self-control, performance of sacrifice, study of the Vedas, austerity, and simplicity.
2.  **The Demonic Qualities (Asuri Sampat)**: Krishna describes the demonic qualities, which include pride, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness, and ignorance. Those who possess the demonic nature do not know what is to be done and what is not to be done. They are unclean, untruthful, and believe that the world is unreal, with no foundation and no God in control.
3.  **The Fate of the Demonic**: Krishna explains that those who are of a demonic nature are cast into the ocean of material existence, where they are born again and again in demonic species of life. They are the lowest of mankind, and they can never approach him.
4.  **The Three Gates to Hell**: Krishna identifies lust, anger, and greed as the three gates to hell. He urges Arjuna to abandon these three and to live a life regulated by the scriptures.

## Key Themes

-   **Divine Qualities**: The virtues that lead to spiritual progress and liberation.
-   **Demonic Qualities**: The vices that lead to bondage and suffering.
-   **Spiritual Progress**: The importance of cultivating divine qualities and abandoning demonic ones.
-   **Moral Guidance**: The clear distinction between right and wrong, and the consequences of each.

## Commentarial Perspectives

-   **Shankara (Advaita)**: Shankara interprets the divine and demonic natures as the products of the three modes of material nature. He sees the chapter as a guide to cultivating sattvic qualities, which are conducive to the attainment of knowledge.
-   **Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)**: Ramanuja sees the divine and demonic natures as the result of one's past karma and the grace of God. He emphasizes that those who take refuge in God can overcome the demonic nature and cultivate the divine qualities.
-   **Madhva (Dvaita)**: Madhva interprets the divine and demonic natures as the inherent qualities of different classes of souls. He maintains that some souls are eternally destined for liberation, while others are eternally destined for damnation.
