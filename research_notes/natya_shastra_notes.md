## Key Information

### Title and Meaning
- **Nāṭya Śāstra** (नाट्य शास्त्र)
- **Nāṭya** = "act, represent" (from root Nata - नट)
- **Śāstra** = "precept, rules, manual, compendium, treatise"
- Literally: "Treatise on the Art of Performance"

### Author and Dating
- **Traditional Attribution**: Sage Bharata (Bharata Muni)
- **Authorship**: Possibly multiple authors, though some scholars argue for single compiler
- **Dating**: Highly debated
  - Estimates range from 500 BCE to 500 CE
  - Most scholars: First complete version between 200 BCE to 200 CE
  - Surviving version likely existed by 8th century CE
  - Text may have started in 1st millennium BCE and expanded over time

### Structure
- **36 chapters** (Adhyaya)
- **~6,000 poetic verses** (shlokas)
- Mix of poetry and prose in some manuscripts
- Multiple manuscript versions with variations

### Historical Roots
- Extends back to **Natasutras** (mid-1st millennium BCE, ~600 BCE)
- Mentioned by Panini (~500 BCE) in his grammar text
- References earlier scholars: Shilalin and Krishashva
- Performers called "Shailalinas" after sage Shilalin

### Comprehensive Coverage

The text covers all aspects of performing arts:

1. **Dramatic Composition**
   - Structure of plays
   - Types of drama
   - Plot construction

2. **Stage and Theatre**
   - Construction of stage
   - Theatre architecture
   - Stage design

3. **Acting**
   - Genres of acting
   - Body movements (angika abhinaya)
   - Facial expressions
   - Gestures (mudras)

4. **Costumes and Makeup**
   - Character types
   - Costume design
   - Makeup techniques

5. **Music**
   - Musical scales (svaras)
   - Musical instruments
   - Integration of music with performance
   - Two genres: Gandharva (formal, ceremonial) and Gana (informal, entertainment)

6. **Dance**
   - Dance movements (nritta)
   - Integration with drama

7. **Art Direction**
   - Role of director
   - Production management

### The Rasa Theory (Core Philosophy)

**Most Important Contribution**: The aesthetic theory of **Rasa** (रस)

**Key Principles**:
- Entertainment is a desired effect but NOT the primary goal
- Primary goal: Transport audience into parallel reality
- Create state of wonder and aesthetic experience
- Allow audience to experience essence of their own consciousness
- Promote spiritual and moral reflection

**The Eight Rasas** (emotional essences):
1. Shringara (love/beauty)
2. Hasya (laughter/mirth)
3. Karuna (compassion/pathos)
4. Raudra (fury/anger)
5. Vira (heroism/courage)
6. Bhayanaka (terror/fear)
7. Bibhatsa (disgust)
8. Adbhuta (wonder/amazement)

(Later tradition added 9th: Shanta - peace/tranquility)

### Significance

**"Fifth Veda"**:
- Often called the Fifth Veda
- Makes Vedic wisdom accessible through arts
- Democratizes spiritual knowledge

**Encyclopedic Treatise**:
- Oldest surviving ancient Indian work on performance arts
- Comprehensive coverage of all performing arts
- Influenced dance, music, and literary traditions across India

**Influence on Classical Arts**:
- Foundation for all Indian classical dance forms (Bharatanatyam, Kathak, Odissi, etc.)
- Basis for Indian classical music theory
- Influenced Sanskrit drama and poetry

### Secondary Literature

**Major Commentary**:
- **Abhinavabharati** (10th century CE)
- Written by Abhinavagupta
- Classic Sanskrit bhasya (commentary)
- Expanded and clarified rasa theory

### Cross-References
- **Agni Purana** - Includes chapters following Natyashastra format
- **Panini's Ashtadhyayi** - Mentions Natasutras
- **Tamil Classics** - Show parallel musical tradition in South India
- **Kalidasa's Works** - Applied Natyashastra principles in drama
- **Abhinavagupta's Tantraloka** - Philosophical connections

### Modern Relevance

**Living Tradition**:
- Still used as foundational text for Indian classical arts
- Taught in dance and music schools
- Basis for theatrical productions
- Influences film and modern performance

**Academic Study**:
- Subject of extensive scholarly research
- Comparative aesthetics
- Performance studies
- Cultural anthropology
